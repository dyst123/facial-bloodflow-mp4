Facial Blood Flow
as a Task Marker

Can we detect what task a person is performing
from their facial blood flow signal?

rPPG MediaPipe FaceMesh Beer-Lambert Pilot Study · n=3
2 / 10
Background

What We're Measuring

  • Blood flow signal extracted from ordinary RGB video — no special hardware
  • MediaPipe FaceMesh detects 478 facial landmarks per frame
  • Four regions tracked: nose, forehead, left cheek, right cheek
  • Signal = mean green channel intensity within each region per frame
  • Green channel chosen because haemoglobin absorbs green light most strongly — this is the rPPG technique
  • Sampling rate: ~35 fps across all recordings
3 / 10
Methods

Signal Processing

1
Green intensity per frame — mean pixel value inside each ROI polygon
2
Beer-Lambert transform — bf = –log(intensity) — more blood = lower reflectance = higher bf value
3
Moving-average smoothing — window of 20 frames to remove high-frequency noise
4
Zero-mean normalisation — subtract mean to get relative change (not absolute blood flow)
4 / 10
Methods

Analysis Pipeline

1
Feature Extraction — RMS amplitude, cardiac band power (0.7–3 Hz), dominant frequency per ROI × trial
2
Cross-Correlation — Pearson r between T1↔T2, T1↔T3, T2↔T3 waveforms per ROI
3
Power Spectral Density — frequency content comparison across trials per ROI
4
Bar Charts — visual summary of RMS and cardiac-band power per trial

Finding 2 — Frequency Content (PSD)

  • T1 dominant frequency: ~0.96–1.1 Hz (~58–66 BPM) in s1, s2
  • T2 and T3 dominant frequency: ~0.82 Hz (~49 BPM) — consistent across all subjects
  • T2 and T3 share the same frequency signature — physiologically similar
  • Shift in dominant freq = change in heart rate or vasomotor rhythm between conditions

Results

Finding 3 — Waveform Similarity

  • All trial pair correlations are low (|r| < 0.30) — waveforms differ in shape, not just amplitude
  • T1 vs T3 is most divergent (forehead r = –0.29 in s1) — T1 and T3 are most physiologically different
  • T2 vs T3 correlations are more positive than T1 vs either — T2 and T3 share a common pattern
  • Pattern consistent across s1, s2, and s3 — not a single-subject artefact
Note on p-values: With ~6,000 data points per trial, even very small correlations reach p < 0.001. The magnitude of r matters more than significance here.

Discussion

Can We Build a Classifier?

Not yet — because

  • n = 3 subjects — model would overfit to individuals
  • Task labels unknown — can't interpret what is being classified
  • BVP/EDA unvalidated — signal may partly reflect artefact
  • One recording per condition — no within-subject repeatability

Roadmap

  • Validate against BVP + EDA (data exists)
  • Run s4–s10 to build sample size
  • Pilot SVM / LDA on 12 features
  • Evaluate with leave-one-subject-out cross-validation
10 / 10
Conclusions

Key Takeaways

  • Facial blood flow differs measurably between tasks — T1 is consistently quieter than T2/T3
  • The effect is seen in amplitude, frequency content, and waveform shape independently
  • The pattern replicates across 3 subjects — not a single-person fluke
  • T2 and T3 are more similar to each other than either is to T1 — suggesting a shared physiological state
  • The signal is promising for classification, but more subjects and BVP/EDA validation are needed first
  • This technique requires no wearable sensors — any RGB camera is sufficient
